# Roux

A roux is a cooked mixture of equal parts fat and flour, used to thicken sauces, soups, and stews. It is one of the most fundamental techniques in cooking.

## Method

1. Melt butter (or other fat) in a heavy-bottomed saucepan over medium heat
2. Add an equal weight of all-purpose flour
3. Whisk continuously until the flour is fully incorporated
4. Continue cooking to the desired stage (see below)

The key is constant stirring. Lumps form when flour hits hot fat without agitation. If lumps do form, whisk vigorously or pass through a sieve.

## Ratios

The classic ratio is **1:1 by weight** — equal parts fat and flour.

| Fat | Flour | Liquid it will thicken |
|-----|-------|----------------------|
| 30g butter | 30g flour | ~500ml (thin sauce) |
| 45g butter | 45g flour | ~500ml (medium sauce) |
| 60g butter | 60g flour | ~500ml (thick sauce) |

For a standard bechamel or white sauce, the medium ratio (45g:45g per 500ml) is most common.

## Stages

A roux changes color and flavor as it cooks:

### White roux (2-3 minutes)
- Pale, no color change
- Mild, slightly nutty
- Used for: bechamel, cream sauces, souffles

### Blond roux (4-5 minutes)
- Light golden color
- Nuttier aroma
- Used for: veloute sauces, lighter gravies

### Brown roux (8-15 minutes)
- Deep amber to chocolate brown
- Rich, toasted, nutty flavor
- Lower thickening power (the longer you cook, the less it thickens)
- Used for: gumbo, dark gravies, espagnole sauce

**Important:** A brown roux has roughly half the thickening power of a white roux. Adjust quantities accordingly.
