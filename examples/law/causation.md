# Causation

Causation links the defendant's [[depends-on::breach]] to the plaintiff's harm. Even if the defendant breached a duty, they are only liable if that breach actually caused the damage.

## But-For Test

The starting point for cause-in-fact:

> "But for the defendant's conduct, would the harm have occurred?"

If the answer is **no** — the harm would not have happened without the defendant's actions — then cause-in-fact is established.

### Limitations

The but-for test fails in cases of:

- **Multiple sufficient causes** — two fires, each capable of destroying a house, merge and destroy it. Neither is a but-for cause alone.
- **Indeterminate defendants** — multiple hunters fire negligently, one pellet hits the plaintiff, but it's impossible to tell whose.

In these cases, courts may use the **substantial factor test**: was the defendant's conduct a substantial factor in causing the harm?

## Proximate Cause

Also called "legal cause." Even if the defendant's conduct was a but-for cause, liability is limited to harms that are **reasonably foreseeable**.

Proximate cause asks: is the connection between the breach and the harm close enough that the defendant should be held responsible?

### Intervening and superseding causes

- **Intervening cause** — an event that occurs between the defendant's breach and the plaintiff's harm. If foreseeable, the defendant is still liable.
- **Superseding cause** — an unforeseeable intervening event that breaks the chain of causation. The defendant is no longer liable.

Example: A driver negligently hits a pedestrian. An ambulance takes the pedestrian to the hospital, where a surgeon commits malpractice. The malpractice is an intervening cause (medical complications are foreseeable). But if the hospital is struck by a meteor, that would be a superseding cause.

## Foreseeability

The scope of liability is bounded by what was foreseeable at the time of the breach:

- **Type of harm** must be foreseeable (not the exact manner or extent)
- The **thin skull rule** (eggshell plaintiff): the defendant takes the plaintiff as they find them. If a minor injury is foreseeable but the plaintiff has a pre-existing condition that makes it severe, the defendant is liable for the full extent
