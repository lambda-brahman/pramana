# Breach of Duty

A breach occurs when a person's conduct falls below the standard of care owed. Once a [[depends-on::duty-of-care]] is established, the question becomes: did the defendant meet that standard?

## Definition

Breach is the gap between the required standard of care and the defendant's actual conduct. It is measured objectively — what matters is what the defendant *did*, not what they *intended*.

## Reasonable Person Standard

The core test: would a **reasonable person** in the same circumstances have acted differently?

The reasonable person is:

- Not perfect, but prudent
- Aware of common risks
- Capable of taking ordinary precautions

The standard adjusts for context — a reasonable surgeon acts differently from a reasonable driver — but it never depends on the defendant's personal shortcomings.

## Determining breach

### The Hand Formula (Judge Learned Hand)

From *United States v. Carroll Towing Co.* (1947):

**B < P x L** means breach occurred, where:

- **B** = burden of taking precautions
- **P** = probability of harm
- **L** = gravity (magnitude) of the harm

If the cost of prevention is less than the expected harm, failing to prevent it is a breach.

### Evidence of breach

- **Custom and practice** — did the defendant follow industry norms? (Not conclusive but relevant)
- **Statutory standards** — violating a safety statute can be breach per se
- **Res ipsa loquitur** — "the thing speaks for itself." When the harm wouldn't normally occur without negligence (e.g., a surgical sponge left inside a patient), breach may be inferred
