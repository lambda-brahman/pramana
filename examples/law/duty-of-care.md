# Duty of Care

Duty of care is the legal obligation to act with reasonable care toward others who might foreseeably be harmed by your actions.

## Definition

A duty of care exists when a person's conduct creates a foreseeable risk of harm to another. The question is: would a reasonable person in the defendant's position have recognized the risk?

Not everyone owes a duty to everyone. The law limits duty based on the relationship between the parties and the foreseeability of harm.

## Tests

Courts use different tests depending on the jurisdiction:

### Foreseeable plaintiff test (Palsgraf)

A defendant owes a duty only to those within the foreseeable "zone of danger." Established in *Palsgraf v. Long Island Railroad Co.* (1928).

### General duty rule (Rowland)

Everyone owes a duty of care to everyone else. The question is whether policy factors justify limiting that duty. From *Rowland v. Christian* (1968), factors include:

- Foreseeability of harm
- Closeness of connection between conduct and injury
- Moral blame
- Policy of preventing future harm
- Burden on the defendant

### Caparo three-stage test (UK)

From *Caparo Industries v. Dickman* (1990):

1. Was the harm foreseeable?
2. Was there sufficient proximity between the parties?
3. Is it fair, just, and reasonable to impose a duty?

## Examples

| Scenario | Duty exists? | Reason |
|----------|-------------|--------|
| Driver on a public road | Yes | All drivers owe care to other road users |
| Landowner to a trespasser | Limited | No duty to make premises safe, but cannot set traps |
| Doctor to a patient | Yes | Professional relationship creates heightened duty |
| Bystander witnessing a drowning | Generally no | No duty to rescue (absent special relationship) |
| Business to its customers | Yes | Invitees are owed a duty to maintain safe premises |
